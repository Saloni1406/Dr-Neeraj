Endometrium Cancer


Endometrial cancer is a type of cancer that starts in the endometrium, the inner lining of the uterus (womb). It most commonly affects postmenopausal women, though it can also occur before menopause. It is the most common cancer of the female reproductive organs. The majority of endometrial cancers are adenocarcinomas, which arise from glandular cells in the endometrium.


Signs and Symptoms


Early detection is possible because symptoms often appear early. Common signs include:
Abnormal vaginal bleeding (most common symptom)
After menopause
Between periods
Heavy or prolonged bleeding
Pelvic pain or pressure
Pain during intercourse
Unusual vaginal discharge (especially if watery or blood-tinged)
Unintended weight loss (in advanced stages)
Fatigue and loss of appetite (in later stages)


Treatment Options


Treatment depends on the stage of the cancer, the type of cells, and the patient’s overall health.
1. Surgery (Primary Treatment) Hysterectomy: Removal of the uterus
Salpingo-oophorectomy: Removal of ovaries and fallopian tubes
Lymph node dissection: To check if cancer has spread
2. Radiation Therapy May be used before or after surgery
External beam radiation or vaginal brachytherapy (internal)
3. Hormone Therapy Used in some low-grade cancers, especially when surgery isn’t an option
Progestins, tamoxifen, or aromatase inhibitors
4. Chemotherapy Often used in advanced or aggressive cancers
Common drugs: Carboplatin and Paclitaxel
5. Targeted Therapy & Immunotherapy For advanced or recurrent cancer
Drugs that target specific mutations or help the immune system recognize cancer cells (e.g., pembrolizumab)


Risk Factors for Endometrial Cancer


Obesity
Hormone imbalance (high estrogen levels)
Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
Diabetes
Family history of uterine or colon cancer (e.g., Lynch syndrome)
Never being pregnant
Early menstruation or late menopause
Use of tamoxifen (for breast cancer)
